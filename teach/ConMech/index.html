Continuum Mechanics

Prerequisites:

A good working knowledge of basic classical mechanics, basic equilibrium statistical mechanics, vector analysis and the definition of Cartesian tensors. Tensor analysis is not necessary.

Syllabus:
  1. Recapitulation of Classical Mechanics, dynamical systems.
  2. Solids, liquids and glasses, scattering and correlation functions.
  3. Elasticity (Feynmann volume II, lecture 38): Stress, Strain, Hooke's law
  4. Kinematics of elastic deformatiton. (Feynmann volume II lecture 39), use of Cartesian tensors.
  5. Equations of an ideal fluid, conservation laws, Eulerian and Lagrangian coordinates (Feynmann volume II, lecture 40)
  6. Viscosity, flow of incompressible fluids/Navier--Stokes equation. (Feynmann volume II, lecture 41)
  7. Flow of incompressible, highly viscous, fluid, Stokes equations, elementary solutions, numerical solutions of Stokes problem in complex geomoery, boundary integral and green's function.
  8. Linearised Navier-Stokes equations, stability and transition in incompressible flows.
  9. Incompressible flows with boundaries, boundary layer theory.
  10. Recapitulation of statistical mechanics.
  11. Hydrodynamics, techniques of coarse-graining, second derivation of dynamical equations of fluids.
  12. Waves in fluids and elastic media, sound.
  13. The problem of pattern formation, method of multiple scales (homogenization),
  14. Scaling and self-similarity, Kolmogorov theory of turbulence and the Sedov-Taylor problem of spherical blast wave.
  15. Non-cartesian tensors, covariant derivatives, elasticity of membranes.
  16. Applications to microfluidics and biophysical fluid dynamics (mechanics of swimming and flying).

Schedule

The course shall be offered during the spring term of 2015. There will be sixteen one-and-half hour lectures and a few exercise/tutorial classes. Each one-and-half hour lecture will be broken up into two consecutive 45-minute lectures with a 5 minute-long gap in between. Typically three classes per week for 5 weeks and a tutorial class per week.

Course Materials

The course shall borrow from many sources:

  • Arnold Sommerfeld: Lectures of theoretical physics, volume II (Mechanics of deformable media). An old classic.
  • The Feynmann Lectures on Physics , volume II, chapters 38 to 41. Available online at this location .
  • Landau and Lifshitz: Course on Theoretical Physics Mostly from volume 6 (Fluid Mechanics) and volume 7 (Elasticity) but also from parts of volume 5 (Statistical Physics Part I) and volume 10 (Physical Kinetics).

    Exam: About 40% on homeworks and 60% on a final exam. The final exam may be an open-book one.
